🌊 Gaussian Processes — Don't Fit Weights, Put a Prior Over Functions Themselves

Click anywhere on the chart to drop observations. Day 36 kept a distribution over the weights of a straight line. A Gaussian Process throws the line away and keeps a distribution over whole functions — no fixed shape at all. You never pick basis functions; you pick a kernel (a notion of "which inputs should give similar outputs") and the maths hands you a posterior mean curve, a shaded ±2σ band that pinches tight where you have data and fattens back to the prior between and beyond points, and actual sampled functions. Every curve is conditioned in your browser with a real Cholesky solve.

The kernel here is the squared-exponential (RBF): k(x,x') = σ_f²·exp(−(x−x')² / 2ℓ²) — two inputs are correlated, and that correlation decays with distance over a length-scale ℓ. Stack it into a matrix and the observations become one big joint Gaussian; conditioning the unseen points on the seen ones is pure linear algebra. Posterior mean μ* = K*(K + σ_n²I)⁻¹y; posterior covariance Σ* = K** − K*(K + σ_n²I)⁻¹K*ᵀ. The diagonal of Σ* is the band; a Cholesky of Σ* draws the wiggly sampled functions. Move the sliders, drop points, and watch the band breathe.

▮ THE KERNEL — correlation as a function of distance
This is the entire model: k(τ) = σ_f²·exp(−τ² / 2ℓ²), the covariance between two outputs whose inputs are a distance τ apart. At τ = 0 it equals the signal variance σ_f² (a point is perfectly correlated with itself); it decays to near-zero past a few length-scales ℓ (dashed line), beyond which the GP treats inputs as effectively independent. Slide ℓ to stretch or squeeze this bell — that single choice is what makes the fit on the left smooth or wiggly.
▮ PREDICTIVE ±σ AS A FUNCTION OF x
The half-width of the band, σ(x*) = √(k(x*,x*) − k*ᵀ(K+σ_n²I)⁻¹k*), plotted across the input range. Unlike Bayesian linear regression — where uncertainty grows without bound — a GP's band dips to a noise-limited minimum at every observation and rises back to a flat ceiling σ_f (dashed purple) between and beyond points: it never claims to know more than the prior where it has no data. 🔨 Build it yourself

    
scikit-learn ships this as GaussianProcessRegressor — hand it a kernel and it even learns the length-scale, signal variance and noise from your data by maximising the marginal likelihood. Below is the whole thing: the kernel, the three kernel matrices, the Cholesky conditioning that produced every mean curve and band you just dragged around, sampling real functions from the posterior, and finally the one-liner.

    
    
      
  1. 
        
    1
    The kernel — similarity as a function of distance
    
        
    A GP is defined entirely by its kernel: the covariance between the outputs at two inputs. The squared-exponential (RBF) kernel says nearby inputs are strongly correlated and that correlation decays smoothly with distance. Two knobs: σ_f² the signal variance (how far the function swings) and ℓ the length-scale (how quickly inputs stop mattering to each other).
    
        
    import numpy as np

# squared-exponential / RBF kernel: nearby inputs -> correlated outputs
def rbf(a, b, sf2=1.0, ell=1.0):
    d = a[:, None] - b[None, :]          # all pairwise input differences
    return sf2 * np.exp(-d**2 / (2 * ell**2))

# sf2 = signal variance  (vertical swing, and the band's height far from data)
# ell = length-scale     (how fast correlation decays with distance)

  2. 
        
    2
    The three kernel matrices
    
        
    Conditioning needs three blocks of one big joint covariance: K between the observed inputs (plus observation noise on its diagonal), K* between the test inputs and the observed ones, and K** between the test inputs themselves. That's it — no design matrix, no weights.
    
        
    X = np.array([-4., -2., 0.5, 3.])      # where you observed
y = np.array([-1.2, 0.8, 0.3, -1.5])   # what you observed
noise = 0.02                           # observation-noise VARIANCE (sigma_n^2)

Xs  = np.linspace(-5, 5, 200)          # test inputs (where we predict)
K   = rbf(X, X)  + noise*np.eye(len(X))   # train-train  (N x N)
Ks  = rbf(Xs, X)                          # test-train   (M x N)
Kss = rbf(Xs, Xs)                         # test-test    (M x M)
    
      
    3. 
      
  3. 
        
    3
    Condition — the posterior mean via a Cholesky solve
    
        
    The posterior mean is μ* = K*(K + σ_n²I)⁻¹y. Never form that inverse directly — factor K = L Lᵀ once with a Cholesky (numerically stable, half the cost) and solve two triangular systems. The vector alpha is reused for every test point. This is the exact solve the demo runs on each click and slider move.
    
        
    L     = np.linalg.cholesky(K)                    # K = L Lᵀ   (lower-triangular)
alpha = np.linalg.solve(L.T, np.linalg.solve(L, y))   # solve (K) alpha = y
mean  = Ks @ alpha                               # posterior mean = K* (K)^-1 y
    
      
    4. 
      
  4. 
        
    4
    The posterior covariance — the ±2σ band
    
        
    The posterior covariance is Σ* = K** − K*(K + σ_n²I)⁻¹K*ᵀ: start from the prior covariance and subtract whatever the data explains. Its diagonal is the per-point variance; the square root, times two, is the shaded band. At an observation the subtracted term nearly cancels the prior, so the band pinches; far away K*→0, nothing is subtracted, and it relaxes back to the prior height σ_f.
    
        
    v    = np.linalg.solve(L, Ks.T)              # solve L v = K*ᵀ   (reuse L)
cov  = Kss - v.T @ v                         # K** - K* (K)^-1 K*ᵀ
std  = np.sqrt(np.clip(np.diag(cov), 0, None))
band_lo, band_hi = mean - 2*std, mean + 2*std   # tight at data, wide between

  5. 
        
    5
    Sample whole functions from the posterior
    
        
    The band summarises the spread; drawing actual functions makes it visceral. A sample is a draw from the joint Gaussian N(mean, Σ*) over the whole test grid — Cholesky-factor Σ* (with a whisper of jitter for stability) and push standard-normal noise through it. These are the grey curves in the demo; watch every one thread exactly through your observations.
    
        
    Ls = np.linalg.cholesky(cov + 1e-6*np.eye(len(Xs)))   # jitter keeps it PD
for _ in range(10):
    f = mean + Ls @ np.random.randn(len(Xs))   # one plausible function
    plt.plot(Xs, f, color="grey", alpha=0.3)
plt.fill_between(Xs, band_lo, band_hi, alpha=0.2)      # the uncertainty band
plt.plot(Xs, mean, color="dodgerblue", lw=2)           # posterior mean curve
plt.scatter(X, y, c="k", zorder=3)                     # the observations

    The one-liner: scikit-learn learns the kernel for you
    
        
    GaussianProcessRegressor does all of the above and tunes the length-scale, signal variance and noise by maximising the log marginal likelihood — no hand-tuning of sliders. Ask for return_std=True and you get calibrated error bars for free. Reach for a GP when data is small-to-medium, smoothness is a fair assumption, and an honest uncertainty band matters (forecasting, active learning, Bayesian optimisation).
    
        
    from sklearn.gaussian_process import GaussianProcessRegressor
from sklearn.gaussian_process.kernels import RBF, WhiteKernel, ConstantKernel as C

kernel = C(1.0) * RBF(length_scale=1.0) + WhiteKernel(noise_level=0.02)
gp = GaussianProcessRegressor(kernel=kernel, normalize_y=True)
gp.fit(X[:, None], y)          # LEARNS sf2, ell, noise by max marginal likelihood
mean, std = gp.predict(Xs[:, None], return_std=True)   # predictions WITH error bars

# GP = Bayesian linear regression with an INFINITE basis (the kernel trick):
#   finite basis phi -> pick a kernel and never write the basis down at all.
# cost is O(N^3) from the Cholesky -> sparse / inducing-point GPs for big N.
# use the band to pick where to sample next -> Bayesian Optimization.

🎉 Day 37 of MachineLearningFromZero done.
You stopped fitting weights and put a prior over functions. A kernel k(x,x') = σ_f²·exp(−(x−x')²/2ℓ²) defined similarity; stacking it made the data one joint Gaussian. Conditioning gave a closed form — mean μ* = K*(K+σ_n²I)⁻¹y, covariance Σ* = K** − K*(K+σ_n²I)⁻¹K*ᵀ — solved stably with a Cholesky. The band pinched to a noise-limited waist at every observation and relaxed to the prior ceiling σ_f in the gaps, never over-claiming where there was no data — the calibrated, any-shape cousin of Day 36's straight-line uncertainty. You sampled real wiggly functions that all thread your points, and saw the length-scale turn the fit smooth or jittery. 👉 Tomorrow — Day 38: Bayesian Optimization — turn this uncertainty band into a decision: use it to pick the single most informative place to sample next, and optimise expensive black-box functions in a handful of evaluations.
